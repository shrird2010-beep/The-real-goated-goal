# The-real-goated-goal
My final attempt of making a portable 12 by 6ft goal.
Goated-goalV2

Inspo: [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eOJQuOstX0s](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=S5OUoixzOfU)

Overview
**The real goated goal** is my final (and honestly best) attempt at building a fully adjustable soccer training goal. It expands up to **12 ft wide and 6 ft tall**, giving me a realistic shooting target without spending a ton of money. Unlike fixed-size goals, Goated Goal can be changed to different widths, which helps a lot when I’m practicing in different spaces.

This project focuses on **mechanical engineering, CADing (yes, I’m still calling it that), and prototyping**. Instead of electronics, the innovation is in the adjustable structure, custom 3D‑printed parts, and off‑the‑shelf poles. After many redesigns, I finally made several new printed parts that make the whole thing stronger and easier to assemble.

---

## Problem with other real-world goals
Most affordable soccer goals are:
- Too small for realistic shooting practice
- Fixed in size and not adjustable
- Flimsy or overpriced
- Annoying to move around (especially through tight spaces)

As a student athlete, I wanted a **full-size training goal** that is:
- Adjustable
- Strong enough to handle hard shots
- Portable and easy to assemble
- Affordable (around $50)

The real goated goal (Goated GoalV2) solves all of these problems with thoughtful mechanical design.

---

## Key Features
- Adjustable width up to **12 ft**
- Custom **3D-printed structural corners and holders**
- Telescoping poles for adjustable sizing
- Impact-resistant full-size net
- Hardware-only/3d printing only so it is easy for anyone to replicate

## Assembly Summary
1. Insert poles into the 3D-printed corner brackets
2. Attach diagonal support poles
3. Bolt together the boltplates
4. Insert the goal inside
5. bolt the poles together
6. Secure the net using the straps 
7. Adjust sizing based on training needs
8. When your done, just pack it up have!


## What I Learned
- Designing **load-bearing 3D-printed parts**
- Accounting for real-world tolerances (I learned this through my brainstorming and potential designs)
- Balancing costs with strength and adjustablity
- Documenting my full project clearly and percisely
